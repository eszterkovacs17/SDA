Introduction

The San Francisco Crime Dataset (2003â€“2024) tracks various crimes, including drug offenses. This data story focuses on how drug-related crimes change over time and geographical location in the city.

1. Drug-Related Crimes Over the Years (2003â€“2024)

One of the most interesting trends is the way drug crimes have changed in San Francisco over the years. The first visualization shows a time-series bar chart of the total number of drug-related crimes recorded each year from 2003 to 2024.

A significant policy shift occurred in October 2010 with the enactment of Senate Bill 1449. This legislation reclassified the possession of up to 28.5 grams of marijuana from a misdemeanor to a civil infraction, punishable by a fine without court appearances or criminal records. Although signed into law in October 2010, this law took effect on January 1, 2011 hence the smaller dip in 2010. [1]

Following the enactment of Proposition 64 in 2016, which legalized adult-use marijuana in California, the state experienced a significant decline in marijuana-related arrests. In 2017, the first year after legalization, overall marijuana arrests dropped by 56% [2]. This downward trend has continued in subsequent years.

Although marijuana is just one element of the drug issue, the observed trends align with the real-world timeline of marijuana legalization.

Now to some other factors that contribute to the trends: Covid and Fentanyl.

It is reasonable to say that early in the COVID-19 pandemic, drug crime rates saw a decline, most likely due to disruptions in supply chains and civilian lockdowns [3]. In California, a significant decrease in arrests was observed as law enforcement focused on pandemic-related issues.

As pandemic restrictions eased, drug-related offenses increased, with rising overdoses and fentanyl-related deaths. By 2021, drug crime rates in San Francisco and other major California cities climbed, prompting the California Department of Public Health to declare a public health emergency [4].

2. Geographic Distribution of Drug Crimes

The second visualization focuses on the geographical aspect of drug-related crimes. Using a map of San Francisco, we plotted locations of drug crimes as a heatmap animation. This map shows that crime is centralized in specific neighborhoods, not spread out evenly across the city.

Drug-related crime in San Francisco is concentrated in four key neighborhoods: the Tenderloin, South of Market (SoMa), Mission District, and Bayview-Hunters Point.

The Tenderloin and SoMa have been known hotspots for drug activity because of high poverty and crowded living conditions. The Mission District and Bayview-Hunters Point are smaller but still noticeable hotspots [5].

The exact reasons behind these concentrations are complex, but contributing factors may include socio-economic differences, historical marginalization, racial demographics, and high-density housing.
